About the lab

Computational reactor physics, from first principles to industry.

The Reactor Physics & Computational Analysis Laboratory (RPL) at KINGS (KEPCO International Nuclear Graduate School), led by Prof. Joo-il Yoon, is dedicated to nuclear reactor physics, computational methods, and advanced core design. Located in Ulsan — the heart of Korea's nuclear energy industry — RPL bridges rigorous academic research with real-world nuclear engineering. Our work spans the full spectrum of reactor physics computation: from first-principles Monte Carlo neutron transport to industry-grade whole-core diffusion solvers, and from traditional PWR core design to innovative Small Modular Reactor concepts, with a strong international team and deep ties to KEPCO Nuclear Fuel.

Our institution

KEPCO International Nuclear Graduate School.

KINGS campus · Seosaeng, Ulsan

RPL is part of KINGS (KEPCO International Nuclear Graduate School) — a specialized graduate school established by Korea Electric Power Corporation (KEPCO) and opened in 2012. It is devoted entirely to educating the next generation of leaders for the global nuclear and energy industries.

Set in Ulsan — the industrial heart of Korea's nuclear power sector — KINGS delivers practice-oriented master's and doctoral programs to an international student body drawn from dozens of countries, working hand-in-hand with the KEPCO group across the full nuclear-power-plant life cycle. This industry-linked setting is what lets RPL carry its computational methods from first principles all the way to real reactor cores.

Research areas

Five directions, one computational core.

01

Monte Carlo Neutron Transport

In-house GPU-accelerated Monte Carlo code (MONTEX), CUDA-based continuous-energy transport, and multigroup cross-section generation.

02

Whole-Core Transport & Diffusion

Two-step Monte Carlo → pin-by-pin diffusion method (MONTEX/SPHINCS) and MOC-based 2D/1D whole-core transport (CROMA).

03

SMR Core Design

Innovative Small Modular Reactor core concepts, soluble-boron-free reactivity control, and APR1400 core design for next-generation systems.

04

Computational Methods

Method of Characteristics (MOC), Coarse-Mesh Finite-Difference (CMFD) acceleration, and depletion methods including predictor-corrector and CRAM.

05

AI & HPC for Nuclear

GPU/CUDA acceleration, MPI/OpenMP parallel frameworks, and machine-learning applications in reactor-physics simulation and optimization.

The code system

A two-step PWR pipeline, plus solvers for new geometries.

Lattice transport and Monte Carlo generate cross sections; HYBRID processes them into tablesets; SPHINCS runs the pin-by-pin whole core. Alongside the PWR chain, we build solvers for hexagonal and cylindrical Gen-IV geometries. Every code shares one foundation — the JIArray numerical library.

Teaching

Graduate courses in reactor physics and computation.

Reactor Physics

Neutron lifecycle, criticality, diffusion theory, and the physics of sustained fission chain reactions in thermal and fast systems.

Nuclear Core Design

Practical APR1400 Nuclear Design (PAND) — hands-on full-cycle design methodology of Korea's flagship advanced PWR.

Computational Methods in Nuclear Engineering

Numerical methods for reactor-physics problems: finite-difference, finite-element, and spectral methods for the transport equations.

Neutron Transport Methods

Stochastic and deterministic simulation: random-number generation, variance reduction, tallying, and parallel implementation.
